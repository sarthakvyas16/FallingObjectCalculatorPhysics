Falling Objects Under Gravity and Air Resistance


Physical Explanations

In our atmosphere, air resistance decreases the acceleration due to gravity ("gravitational acceleration") quadratically with increasing fall speed, until a constant terminal velocity is reached. At this maximum speed, the forces of air resistance and gravity are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, so no further acceleration occurs.

This tool calculates the terminal velocity and fall duration. It can display the instantaneous height, velocity, force, and acceleration as a table or as functions in a coordinate system.

The calculation requires several specific parameters, which are briefly explained here:

The mass (symbol:ùëö) m, kilograms : (kg) Mass is a property of matter and a measure of how strongly an object is influenced by gravitational fields and how much it resists acceleration (inertia: the tendency of an object to remain in its current state of motion unless acted upon by external forces).

Cross-Sectional Area keyboard_arrow_down

Cross-Sectional Area (symbol: A, Unit: m2) is a two-dimensional measure of the size of the area exposed to wind (frontal area in vehicles) of a moving object.

drag coefficient keyboard_arrow_down

The drag coefficient (symbol cw) or also known as the drag coefficient, is dimensionless and quantifies the aerodynamic efficiency (streamlining) of a shape, i.e., how well the air can flow past the falling object. The cw-Typical values for different body shapes are approximately as follows:

  • Pinguin: 0.03
  • Drop shape (streamlined body): 0.05
  • Skydiver (vertical position): 0.1
  • Sphere: 0.2 - 0.45
  • Skydiver (horizontal position): 0.28
  • Smart car: 0.37
  • Paraglider: 0.6
  • Motorcycle: 0.7
  • Human (standing): 0.78
  • Truck: 0.8
  • Parachute: 0.9 - 1.4
  • Thin flat plate (perpendicular to the flow): 1.11
